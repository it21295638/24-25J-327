Early interventions for dyslexic children have shown significant benefits, especially when tailored to their native language and cognitive profiles. In Sri Lanka, research points to a gap in digital solutions for dyslexic students using Sinhala, with most existing apps focusing on English.

Children with dyslexia face a range of challenges in reading and writing, especially with complex scripts like Sinhala. Most international research and intervention are focused on English-speaking populations, while local Sri Lankan studies emphasize the urgent need for culturally adapted, digital and multisensory tools for early literacy support.

Recent advances globally have included AI-powered handwriting recognition, speech feedback, and gamified activities, but there is a lack of such tools for Sinhala learners. Local studies confirm that early, adaptive, and interactive support using technology—such as real-time feedback on writing and pronunciation, gamified learning, and progress tracking—significantly improves learning outcomes and engagement. Despite this, there are few comprehensive, accessible mobile applications designed specifically for young dyslexic students in Sri Lanka.

This project aims to bridge that gap by integrating evidence-based educational technology with local language, curriculum, and the cultural context of Sri Lankan classrooms.

Existing tools rarely support adaptive Sinhala learning. Few integrate real-time feedback, gamification, or progress analytics for Sri Lankan children.

Globally, most research on digital support for dyslexia targets English or alphabetic languages, leaving children who learn in Sinhala with limited solutions. The gap is wider for mobile apps that combine handwriting, pronunciation, and adaptive feedback for early grades in the Sri Lankan context. Most e-learning apps are not personalized or locally relevant. Our project directly addresses this by developing an app customized to the needs of Sinhala-speaking dyslexic students, with teacher/parent dashboards and progress analytics.

  • Improve literacy for Sinhala dyslexic students.
  • Provide AI-driven adaptive learning.
  • Enhance early literacy and cognitive skills of Sinhala-speaking dyslexic children using technology-based, evidence-driven interventions.
  • Provide real-time, actionable feedback on handwriting and pronunciation using AI (CNN, EfficientNet, ResNet, Random Forest).
  • Deliver adaptive learning paths tailored to each child’s strengths, weaknesses, and engagement.
  • Support teachers and parents with actionable progress dashboards and analytics.
  • Promote social inclusion and reduce stigma associated with dyslexia in Sri Lankan classrooms.
  • Mobile app for Grade 1 Sinhala dyslexic students.
  • Development of a mobile application for Grade 1 Sinhala-speaking dyslexic students.
  • Handwriting, memory, and pronunciation modules with AI-driven assessment and feedback.
  • Gamified and multi-sensory activities (visual, auditory, tactile) to boost engagement and retention.
  • Personalized learning activities, adaptive difficulty, and real-time monitoring.
  • Progress dashboards for students, teachers, and parents.
  • All content and activities localized for Sri Lankan curriculum and cultural context.
  • AI handwriting & pronunciation, gamification, dashboards...
  • Handwriting Module: Sinhala letter tracing, shape drawing, AI feedback, and camera input.
  • Speech Module: Pronunciation and syllable practice, instant feedback with 1D CNN & MFCC.
  • Adaptive Module: Scrambled sentences, picture storytelling, real-time engagement analysis (ResNet50).
  • Gamification: Badges, rewards, and progress animations.
  • Dashboard: Real-time progress tracking for teachers/parents.
  • Grade 1, Sinhala only. Android device needed.
  • Scope limited to Grade 1 and Sinhala language.
  • Android device with camera and microphone required.
  • No in-app purchases or external integrations in the pilot phase.
